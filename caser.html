Overview

Client

Caser Asesores Financieros is made up by a group of experts in finances with focus on three core areas: profit, flexibility and transparency. They offer their clients a wide range of financial products to help them grow their assets.

Problem

Caser Asesores Financieros wanted to get their webapp redesigned in order to offer their users a fresher and newer look and feel. For this purpose, first, I needed to conduct some research, after which I found out users did not feel comfortable enough anymore with the design of the webapp, as they considered difficult to manage some tasks. This, in turn, translated into a negative joy of use.

Goals

Before setting my goals, I needed to know the key aspects to highlight in a redesign and what questions to pose before proceding:

  • As a business or company, what do we expect our redesign to convey to our users?
  • What‚Äôs the budget limit and how does it affect to the redesign process?
  • To what extent do the current brand guidelines limit the new redesign?

The redesign process needed to be something simple yet at the same time it needed to look like something completely new. Also, we needed to ensure users felt the new design familiar (Nielsen, 2000)

A more specific set of goals would be:

  • Provide users with a newer and fresher design. It needed to feel brand new..
  • Change the Information Architecture as little as possible (only when extrictly needed for usabilty matters).
  • Maintain familiarity to not create a disruptive design for the user (Jacob‚Äôs law).
  • Stick to the brand guidelines.

Process

For this project, I structured a custom working plan into five phases, following a mix between the Design Thinking and Double Diamond Theory approaches.

  • The first step was Audit, where I wanted to know to what extent what we had differed from what we needed.
  • The second step was Research. As what we had differed from what we needed (hence the redesign project) then I had to gather all the possible information.
  • The third step was UX Conception: Once I got the information I needed, I needed to know how I should organize it.
  • The fourth one was the UI Conception, where I came up with wireframes, moodboards and ideas of what the new design should look like, that is, styling the information.
  • In the last phase, Testing, I conducted a Usability Test through Useberry and some further iterations to UI Conception, where I came up with wireframes, moodboards and ideas of what the new design should look like, that is, improve the information.

In this custom process, everything was included into two bigger stages:

  • First, we had what we didn‚Äôt know yet and how could it be. This is a broader stage that included the steps of Audit and Research, which helped us narrow down our choices.
  • Second, when we do know what to do, by organising the information to understand how the final result should be, through the steps of UX Conception, UI Conception and Testing.

Audit

My goal in this phase was to review the current product and learn more about the goals of our client, their user needs, the possible limitations and some key aspects before proceeding. In short, I wanted to know more about my client and the final user‚Äôs needs to know if they were aligned.

Through this UX Audit, I was able to pinpoint some weaknesses and threats and how I could turn them into strenghts and opportunities through a redesign. This phase consisted of two stages:

  • Stakeholders Interview
  • Heuristic Evaluation

Business Goals: Stakesholder Interview

Before I started with analyzing their current webapp, I first needed to know their brand vision and business goals to know to what extent they differed from the current digital product.

To do this, I had a virtual meeting with some of the stakeholders, where I conducted a short interview of five (5) questions that I considered crucial to understand better the starting situation. These questions helped me have a rough idea of what the expected outcome and user persona should be.

Heuristic Evaluation

The next step in the audit phase was contucting a Heuristic Evaluation that allowed me to identify painpoints and usability issues. A heuristic evaluation consists in evaluating a digital product to know how friendly the visual interface is to users and how much of a coherence the design does have.

For this project, I used Jacob Nielsen‚Äôs 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design, a broad rules of thumb for interaction design.

This evaluation was conducted by three (3) people in the design team (it is recommended three to five people because one only evaluator cannot find all the problems) (Nielsen, 1994).

üëÜüèº

Rules for Heuristic Evaluation, by Jakob Nielsen (1994)

Through this evaluation we noticed some issues that were contributing into a negative user experience, among others and according to Jacob Nielsen‚Äôs 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design:

  • #1: Visibility of system status

    The states of some buttons were not understandable enough. We couldn‚Äôt differ well from active, focus and hover.

    Explanation: This rule aims to help users know what they‚Äôre doing through constant feedback. Eg.: If we click a button, we need to know that button is clicked.

  • #2: Match between system and the real world

    Some of the copy was not the best choice available. We noticed some words worked better than others in the menus.

    Explanation: This rule aims to never assume that our understanding of words or concepts will match those of the users.

  • #3: User control and freedom

    We missed a Cancel button in some of the screens.

    Explanation: This rule aims to make sure the exit is clearly labeled and discoverable. Users must be free to choose whether or not to continue.

  • #4: Consistency and standards

    We noticed the design of some tabs was the same as those of the buttons. Also, some of the colors in their corporate website did not specifically match those in the app. This could lead users to mistake.

    Explanation: This rule aims to maintain consistency within a single product.

  • #7: Flexibility and efficiency of use

    We made that some fields automatically filled in with the information given by the user in previous screens, so they didn‚Äôt have to fill in these fields again.

    Explanation: This rule aims to provide accelerators like keyboard shortcuts to make the flow more quick and efficient.

  • #8: Aesthetic and minimalist design

    TWe felt the user interface was obsolete and did not stick to current design trends.

    Explanation: This rule aims to keep the content and visual design of UI focus on the essentials.

Research

Competitive Analysis

After doing general research, I monitored several competitors, although I focused on two of them to know what they were doing and how successful they were in some fields. These fields were decided along with stakeholders and Product Managers. Through this step, I could pinpoint some strenghts and weaknesses of our competitors.

üëÜüèº

Competitive Analysis

Some of the conclusions were that other competitors in the market might be better stablished, with a clearer user interface in their apps. Their system also offered rich options for experienced investors to check their portfolio. However, some of these platforms lacked: a fully dedicated customer service for people that only want to invest money and are not interested in any other type of product (one-to-one service), a well-developed live and in-person support and other ways to check the evolution of assets through history. We could turn others weaknesses into strenghts, and use these new features to create value through design and functionality for stakeholders and final users.

User Survey

I conducted a user survey to 10 customers from Caser‚Äôs database to find out to what extent our hypotheses and findings in the Heuristic Evaluation could be biased. This survey was made on Optimal Workshop and included, among others, questions like:

  • Rate from 1 to 5 your experience when using Caser webapp.
  • Which difficulties do you face when using the app?
  • What motivates you to use the service?
  • In a scale from 1 to 5, where 1 means ‚ÄòVery bad‚Äô and 5 means ‚ÄòExcellent‚Äô, how easy to use do you find the app?
  • What are the reasons that might stop you using the service?
  • Rate from 1 to 5, where 1 means ‚ÄòI don‚Äôt agree‚Äô and 5 means ‚ÄòI do completely agree‚Äô: sometimes, I mistake some buttons.
  • Rate from 1 to 5, where 1 means ‚ÄòI don‚Äôt agree‚Äô and 5 means ‚ÄòI do completely agree‚Äô: sometimes, I get stuck because I can‚Äôt find a way to go back to the previous step.

User Persona

As with other finance products I have conducted research on, we can add some assumptions to make our user persona even more specific, like:

  • All users need to be aged 18 or above 18.
  • All genders.
  • All nationalities.

Furthermore, something else that I learnt to be important to define personas in fintech products is users‚Äô behaviour towards. According to Jea Yu, we can find three types: nomads, hunters and quality seekers (Yu, 2017).

We could confirm by asking directly to some of the stakeholders and Product Managers that the vast majority of their clients are quality seekers, as they generally prefer to have direct phone live assistance and in-person meetings. They usually stay loyal to the firm too.

Arturo

Quality Seeker

Arturo is a Business Administration turned entrepreneur. He founded a software startup that was absorbed by a bigger entity. Now, he stays as one of the silent partners in the company, taking active decisions. He has started looking for other new small companies to invest his money as a business angel, aiming to make more profit and grow his assets.

As a self-made former CEO of a company, Arturo values thorough-developed products that are easy to use, with a premium feeling and design. In his mind, a digital product would require these features in order to be successful.

UX Conception

User Flow

I designed a User Flow based on the User Persona and the painpoints I came across during the Research phase. I also bore in mind previous steps as Competitive Analysis, User Survey and Card Sorting to make sure not to miss any option that was key for the final user.

The main goal was to design a User Flow with all the information in as many screens as possible, ensuring to deliver a simple and easy-to-use architecture while focusing on cost optimization. This would allow me to apply a minimal and elegant design later, sticking to the brand guidelines.

First Usability Test

After validating several times with stakeholders and Product Manager our wirframing process, I thought it was time to make the first Usability Test. In this test, we got pretty close to the metrics we wanted to, but we needed still to improve the design in some areas, speacially, when analyzing the Click Tracking.

First Usability Test for Caser

In this first test, we obtained a 4% misclick rate. We were not far from our objective but there was still some work to do. We made the clickable zones more noticeable by slightly increasing their spacing. We also worked on the readability of the clickable elements by increasing their contrast.

Design System & UI Components Library

After wireframing and getting some feedback from stakeholders and Product Managers, we started to work in the Design System of the new design and a library of UI components in Figma so that developers could have a reference. We needed to stick to the brand guidelines and primary colors. However, we had freedom to modify shapes, some colors, typografies, margins and spaces as long as it didn‚Äôt differ abruptly from the current branding to offer users something new yet familiar (Jacob's Law, 2020).

For the UI Design I researched several Design Systems to know good design practices: IBM‚Äôs Carbon Design System, iOS Human Interface Guidelines, Atlassian‚Äôs Design System and others.

To create the new visual guidelines, I used atomic design methodology. This methodology is made of five stages that work together to create a user interface in a more hierarchical way. It divides in five stages (Frost, 2013):

  • Atoms: Basic elements such as form labels, inputs and buttons.
  • Molecules: Simple groups of design elements that function together as a unit.
  • Organisms: Relatively complex components that form distinct sections of an interface.
  • Templates: Groups of organisms that function together and demonstrate the page‚Äôs underlying content structure.
  • Pages: The highest level of fidelity that show what the interface will look like with real representative content in place.

Project Takeaways

Some of the things I could notice throughout this project were:

  • We only can improve what we can measure: This project is a good example for how we could improve our misclick rate by conducting more than one Usability Testing. I noticed that we can only improve what‚Äôs measurable. Otherwise, we do not have instances to base our work on.
  • Redesign is not easy work: Thanks to this project I learnt how to conduct a redesign that is based on data and research, not only on design trends. Not only we managed to deliver an aesthetic product, but also exactly what was best for users and stakeholders, fulfilling their needs.
