Expenses Wallet
In-App Landing

Growth hacked and raised conversion of accounts
and cards created through design iterations.

Reply time: within 1 hour

Overview

Context

Company description

Factorial is an all-in-one HR software that allows companies turning manual processes into automated ones.

The app offers a solution for companies to manage their employees' expenses: Factorial Expenses.

Initial background

To provide added value and allow companies automating their expense reporting process, we released Factorial Cards in April 2022.

To use cards, customers needed to create first an Expenses Wallet, which provided them with an IBAN, so they could add funds to their wallets and issue new cards.

Problem Solving

Goal What

We needed to raise conversion of Expenses Wallet and Factorial Cards, as well as to decrease the number of drops in the process.

Problem Why

5 months after the feature launch, the amount of accounts and cards created was not as initially expected. We needed to understand why, to fix it and reach the OKRs. This would ultimately allow us to provide the desired value for customers and stakeholders.

Solution How

I took the role of Product Design and Product Management. I decided to structure the workflow as follows:

  1. Firstly, I generated hypotheses and explored the funnel to have an overview of the problem.
  2. Secondly, I interviewed a small group of customers, to understand common painpoints.
  3. Thirdly, I debriefed the information from the interviews, to gather key learnings.
  4. Fourthly, I benchmarked some competitors, and finally validated initial hypotheses.
  5. Fifthly, I wrote the Product Requirements, and share them with stakeholders and the team.
  6. Finally, I designed the final solution and defined the interactive animations.

In-depth Review

Hypotheses Generation

We had launched the solution of Factorial Cards in April 2022. Since then, 5 months had passed and we barely saw conversion. Up to that moment, we had a total of 76 cards created, from 119 accounts.

To have a rough idea about where the problem could be deriving from before we incurred in any development costs, I started to formulate hypotheses, among which I concluded:

  • There wasn't a clear value proposition.
  • We weren't offering support.
  • We weren't highlighting key benefits:
    • Benefits of creating an account.
    • Benefits of creating a card.
    • Benefits of using a card.
  • Customers missed some guidance:
    • How the account works.
    • How Factorial Cards work.
  • Design wasn't generating engagement.

Funnel Exploration

I had a look at the funnel to review the accounts that were dropping in the process and made some discoveries:

  • Customers visited the page but would not start the process.
  • Customers visited the page, started the process, but then dropped.
👆🏼

Generation of Hypotheses

Qualitative research

Once I had formulated the hypotheses, in order to validate them and understand what was really happening, I jumped into intervewing a small group of 10 customers that were using Factorial but had dropped when creating their Expenses Wallet account.

I chose interviews to get direct feedback from customers. I decided to go with a set of open questions similar to the following, to try avoid biased answers:

  • Rapport
    • We've seen you recently started creating an Expenses Wallet account.
  • Build
    • How did you find the process?
  • Peak
    • Was there any information you were missing?
    • Did you find any obstacles?
  • Open feedback
    • Were you to take on the process again, what would you change?
    • From 1 to 5, how would you rate the overall experience? (1 being Too bad and 5 being Excelent?

Defriefing and Key Learnings

After conducting the interviews I noticed customers answered in similar ways and had common painpoints, which matched with the initial hypotheses.

Among these painpoints, I found that nearly every customer had experienced the following:

  • What is it: They wanted to know what the process of creating an account consisted on, and why they needed it.
  • Cost: They wanted to know how much it was going to cost them to use Factorial Cards.
  • Transaction fees: They wanted to know any implicit fees when paying with Factorial Cards, such as Exchange rate, Payments outside EU…
  • Key benefits: They wondered what the ultimate goal of using Factorial Cards was, specially in cases where they were already using Bank Cards.
  • Effort: They wanted to know how long it was going to take to create an account. They were also worried about the learning curve in cases where they were already using another software to report expenses.

Benchmarking & UX Audit

After gathering the common painpoints and the key learnings, I conducted a Benchmark and a UX Audit on some of our closest competitors, according to our business model.

The aim of this was to validate what information competitors were chosing to display, and whether or not this information had any direct relation with the answers customers had given.

Benchmarking and UX Audit

Product Requirements Definition

Once I had generated and validated the different hypotheses through Research and Benchmarking, I started writing the Product Requirements Definition.

In this phase, I gathered all the insights that had helped me validate the Feature Opportunity, and then communicated them to stakeholders and cross-functional teams (Product Marketing and Engineering).

At this point, together with Product Marketing, we also decided to include in the definition of the product a user physchology framework (ELMR framework) that could help me understand better how customers made decissions and what their core desires could look like. This, ultimately, would help me take better design decissions.

ELMR Framework

Through the ELMR Framework I aimed to answer questions like:

  • How can we improve retention?
  • How can we improve life-time value?
  • How can we improve virality?
  • Can we improve any other variables?

After reviewing the ELMR Framework in our Reforge program, I understood that users start the decission-making process with emotion, not logic. It's emotions that drive users towards the action and, then, they end up using logic to justify emotions. But logic rarely leads the way.

Once the user makes the decission, they will seek a reward because they look to confirm that they made the right decision (a reward increases the chance that they will take that action or decision again on a repeated basis).

This helped me understand better how to carry on with the Product Design phase and how to modularize the elements:

  • In the first place, I chose to display a section with the value proposition and a trigger to generate engagement ("Create your Expenses Wallet for free").
  • In the second place, I chose to display a section to communicate the key benefits of using Factorial Cards ("Gain time, Set budgets, Pay Safely…").
  • Finally, I chose to display a section with a brief how-to on what the process looked like in 3 easy steps.

User Stories & Acceptance Criteria

In the design phase, there were several case scenarios that needed to be taken into account. In order to assure quality, I wrote user stories & acceptance criteria following the Gherking methodology:

   
    Given  __________
    When   __________
    And    __________
    Then   __________

In particular, there were two main case scenarios:

  • Customers who did not have an account created, who were based in Spain.
  • Customers who did not have an account created, who were based somewhere other than Spain.

However, there were also some side case scenarios, like:

  • Customers have created and enabled their account, but they're missing some information.
  • Customers have started created their account, but have dropped in the process.
  • Customers have successfully created their account, but need to add an external IBAN to use the feature.

Writing user stories and acceptance criteria served me well to ensure all case scenarios where covered in the final design.

Product Design

Once I wrote the user stories and spotted all possible case scenarios, I proceeded to design the landing page, applying the elements in the Design System.

However, I had some bandwith to define new elements that did not exist, such as the graphics for the Physical and Virtual cards (up to that moment, we didn't have any graphical representation of Factorial Cards inside the product).

In this phase, I also explored the interactive animations, using the library Framer Motion. Together with the Engineering team, we defined the behaviour of these animations. Then, I jumped and helped adjust all the parameteres of the animations for a optimal result.

To do so, I enabled a Gitpod workspace and created a new branch to perform the changes. Then I commited the changes after all parameters had been polished and merged the changes into the main branch of the app.

Success Metrics

Within the first 5 months after Factorial Cards were released, there were 119 accounts and only 76 cards. After the implementation of the new design, we generated 119 new accounts, and x4.5 times new cards created: 343 cards.
